Trailing Arm and Rear Upright

Lightweight suspension member for an offroad car

skills

mechanical engineering, cad, manufacturing

role

designer

client

baja sae


The trailing arm and rear upright supports the entire rear of the car by connecting the frame, wheel, suspension geometry, powertrain and shock.

My main goal was to simplify manufacturing by reducing 4 parts and 12 weld lines while reducing weight. Our team only had two welders, so reducing weld lines means quicker production and less stress on our student welders. Reducing weight is especially important because any weight that moves with the suspension affects the suspension dynamics and requires a stronger spring to effectively keep the wheels on the ground.


Key Learnings

The trailing arm was my most time intensive, stereotypical mechanical engineering project. I taught myself FEA and learned how to optimize for weight based on the results. This project also taught me how to design within a complex system of multiple interfacing components. As all the components were worked on in parallel, I had to continuously meet with other engineers to best design the entire suspension to meet our projectâ€™s individual needs. I also had to design around sizing and location constraints.


My Work

FEA

After simulating three different loads, I continued to cut out weight from the trailing arm while maintaining a safety factor of 1.4. I achieved an 8% weight reduction from the previous year by cutting out 2.9 ounces in each trailing arm and more from fastener reductions.

Manufacturing Difficulties

Due to warpage from welding, I had to refinish the bearing surfaces on the uprights. I designed and manufactured a custom fixture to hold the part while I used a conversational mill to remake the surfaces and snap ring grooves.

Testing

So far the trailing arm has lasted through three competitions (over 20 hours of driving over jumps and bumps) without any signs of wear or failure.
